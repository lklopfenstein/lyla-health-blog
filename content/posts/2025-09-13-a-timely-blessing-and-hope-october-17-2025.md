Catching up here... This whole journaling thing has a bit of a snowballing effect - the longer I go without sharing, the more stuff there is to share. When the posts are going to be larger, it's harder to find the time to sit down and write it all out. But here we are - and here we go... :) 

**Lyla update:**

**SEEG:
**We had the SEEG (invasive EEG that involved inserting 18 probes into the brain to better localize the origin of seizures) back in mid to late August. As a tech enthusiast, it was truly fascinating. As Lyla's Dad... a bit frightening. Cutting to the chase here, they recorded 57 seizures. 56 of those originated around the same area as her last brain surgery, and 1 originated in a different/new but related area. This was instrumental in creating a split in the surgical board meeting in two ways: 1) Is it Rasmussen's? and 2) What is the course of action?

For the first split, most everyone believes that she has "Atypical Rasmussen's". Rasmussen's that doesn't follow the typical patterns. Perhaps a 99%/1% split. 

For the second split, it was more 80%/20% split on the course of action with 80% in favor of a second resection (digging a deeper hole), and 20% in favor of a hemispherectomy (disconnecting the hemispheres leaving her weak or paralyzed on her right side, along with loss of speech and some vision). 

One key element is that not all opinions are weighted the same - the one voice sticking to their guns that it is NOT Rasmussen's is the renowned neurosurgeon that patients travel from overseas to see specifically. After some careful deliberation, we both agreed that a hemispherectomy is too much - we're not there yet. Not ready to wave the white flag and take away a good amount of function to stop the seizures when the proof of Rasmussen's isn't clear cut. We scheduled her second brain resection surgery for October 24th.

One area of concern since the end of the SEEG was that if we remove more of her brain, what function(s) are we losing? We were specifically concerned about taking away her ability to speak, so they performed a "Wada" test toward the end of September. Here are the stakes: Apparently, if we take away her language, language can "move" to the opposite side of the brain, but could be weeks before she speaks at all, and months before she strings a sentence together, and possibly would never be a complete transfer.

**Wada:**
This test is another technologically fascinating one - they put Lyla to sleep, give her an injection that puts half of her brain to sleep, then wake her up to see what function she has and if she's able to talk. They then repeat the process for the opposite side of the brain. One parent was able to be in the room, so Kayla went. From what she describes, it was difficult to tell, but seemed like her language was on the left side (where the seizures are coming from). 

Let me pause there because that's where we've been living for the past 3 weeks or so. Is this the right move?? It looks like we'll take away her ability to speak in favor of stopping the seizures. The team is giving us a mixed bag of answers in regard to what that language transfer process looks like. What if it doesn't work? What if the transfer isn't complete? Or maybe fails to transfer at all? Tears, sleepless nights, difficulty breathing, you name it. 

And then... our pre-op appointments all happened on Wednesday (Oct. 15th), with our timely blessing and hope coming straight from the mouth of the 1% - "I don't plan on touching her language. I believe she will wake up after surgery and be able to talk."

WE'LL TAKE THAT!!!
