What a whirlwind of a weekend. The amount of prayer, support, and love for Lyla and our family has been intense and astounding. We've heard from the closest of friends and family and also complete strangers. All of that support has been meaningful in different ways. Prayers offered up from a close family member have the added context of our everyday battles and struggles. Prayers offered from a stranger don't have the everyday context, but rather a love and care that emerges without the relationship first. It begs the question, "But why?? Why do they care about our family at all??" It's humbling and so incredibly meaningful in a different way - I'm equally grateful for both!!

I can't believe how surprisingly smooth everything has gone. We were discharged 48 hours after surgery ended, and we were home in time for dinner tonight (Sunday). We were expecting to be at the hospital for a week or so. Kay and I had thoughts overtaking our minds that we were destined to be bringing home a girl that was quite different from the one we brought to the hospital, whether that be her ability to speak, fluency, etc. Something. As her parents, Kay and I agree wholeheartedly that we cannot tell a single difference yet. The big thing being her speech - we literally cannot tell a difference before and after. Sure, our surgeon told us that he believed she would talk after surgery, but there were certainly other doctors giving us opposing opinions. Her personality and Lyla-isms, her sense of humor, her fluency... ALL of it remains intact - a miracle in itself. Her first words post-surgery happened within the first hour in response to me telling her "We love you, sweetie". She was groggy/sleepy, but responded with a very clear, "I love you too". Perhaps one of the most beautiful instances of that phrase that I've ever heard.

She's successfully made it through TWO brain surgeries now (or FOUR if you count the insertion and extraction surgeries for her SEEG), and we can't help but draw some comparisons between the two: 

- Shortly after her first surgery, [she was putting this block puzzle thing together](https://www.facebook.com/share/v/1BS1jFQJ6D/) and that was mind blowing to me at the time. After this one, she's at home 48 hours later, [putting together an age appropriate Lego set](https://www.facebook.com/share/v/1CsKLoftUi/) with 260 pieces, visualizing it properly and building it all on her own.

- We didn't have time to think about the first one. This one, we over-analyzed like crazy and had plenty to fear. To the point where during her surgery, Kayla and I had to admit to each other that even with our surgeon's assurance that he believed she'd be able to talk after surgery, we might be taking home a girl that has lost a lot of ability. 

- My favorite difference right now is this: The drive home after the first surgery was very metaphorical and symbolic with the sunset and different songs that played during the drive. Very emotional and intense relief. This time, I was struck by how incredibly ordinary it all was. No sunset, no fanfare, no traffic, etc. Like... everything was just so smooth that it had us just waiting for the other shoe to drop - Lyla did so well with surgery, she's home early, literally zero difference in ability that we can see, and possibly... lower seizure count? Time will tell. 

So what's that all about?? We've gotten so used to being against the odds, worst possible outcomes, etc. that it just felt so out of place. It got me thinking... Perhaps life is a series of miracles in the ordinary, dull, everyday moments? I mean, maybe looking back on it all is easier to see the, ahem... coincidences:

- We lived in Cleveland, so Cleveland Clinic was an easy place to turn for us. Traffic patterns, etc. were familiar
- I worked for a few years at Case Western Reserve University - specifically built out a form-based system for Clinical Evaluations at their "Health Education Campus" for their School of Nursing - our nurse attended there and used that system.
- My Mom and Aunt attended the same high school as our Neurosurgeon (Small world!!). People travel from all over the world to have there surgery completed by him. He is the "1% voice" that I referenced in a prior post that is fighting against the diagnosis of Rasmussen's. 
- The same Neurosurgeon has such experience with brain surgery and Rasmussen's, that when he took out the brain tissue and held it in his hands, he told us that the way the tissue feels... doesn't feel like Rasmussen's does.
- Kayla held a summer job when we first moved up to Cleveland doing catering... at Cleveland Clinic. Catering took her literally to every nook and cranny of the hospital, so she already had familiarity with every building and area.
- and many, many more that I don't have the capacity to write about at the moment. :) 

Most of these points are uneventful or maybe coincidental on their own, but put them together and you've got what I'm gonna call a miracle. 

I'm challenging myself now to notice more of the uneventful everyday moments. Perhaps that'll open my eyes to see more of the beauty of God and His design. 

A lesson for me: **We can't escape God's presence. Not even the ordinary life stuff.** Heck... is ANY of life "ordinary"??
