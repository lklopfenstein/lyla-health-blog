An update!

February was a bit of a blur for us Klops.  We left our (not home) home on February 5th for Minnesota in search of a second opinion and hopefully some answers and came back 10 days later on the 15th.  Perhaps it's appropriate that I'm just now getting to writing it down 10 days after that.  If Phoenix, AZ was the hottest temperature I've ever experienced (118F), then Rochester, MN was certainly the coldest (-18F without windchill!!).  It was crazy the toll it was starting to take on our skin!  Kudos to those that are able to tough it out by living there.  Anyways, on to the update.

**A few numbers:**

- Cleveland has said they're about 90% certain Lyla has Rasmussen's Encephalitis

- Unanimously decided that surgery isn't a good next option - we need to give IVIg a fair shake to see if it'll work - giving it 6 total months to see what happens

- They've also given IVIg about a 10% chance of success 

Mayo Clinic (Rochester, MN) ran their gamut of tests (Baseline EEG, Extended Video EEG, and fMRI), and since we extended our stay by several days, decided to run the IVIg infusion while we were there.  

IVIg infusion went GREAT, thank goodness!  They slowed the infusion rate to 8 hours, and provided fluids before and after as well as Steroids, Benadryl, and Tylenol beforehand to ensure she wouldn't react like she did with the first iteration.  She had rosy cheeks the next day, but felt great - We'll take it!!!

Everyone seems to be in agreement that surgery is a risky option due to the affected hemisphere being the left side, and likely her dominant side.  Her language is likely in her dominant hemisphere, and that's something we don't want to touch - especially given the deficits we've already seen post-surgery.  To be sure, they ordered an "fMRI", or "Functional MRI".  Basically, they provide you instructions during an MRI to see what parts of the brain are activated when you perform certain tasks.

As a result, the only side that "lit up" for language was the right side, so that's good, but possibly not the whole story.  They did mention that she could be "bi-lateral" with language in both sides, but perhaps masked on the left due to the surgery.  If language were completely on the right though, it would make the surgical options a bit less risky.

**The two working theories remain:**

- Rasmussen's Encephalitis

- Another resection surgery is necessary - more needs taken out

Mayo Clinic is less certain than 90% that it is Rasmussen's - they mentioned that there are a few things that point toward Rasmussen's, but also a few things that point away from it.  They won't really have much more information until the surgical board discusses her case in early-mid April. 

So we wait... nothing new here, right? :)
