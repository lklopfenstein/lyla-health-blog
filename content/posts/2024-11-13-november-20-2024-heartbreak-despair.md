Howdy folks!  I'll just get right into it.  

We had the long awaited follow up MRI on November 7th to see how things have been going inside that head of hers.  Turns out... not so great.  They were able to see atrophy of her brain, which points to Rasmussen's Encephalitis ([Click here to read all about it](https://www.encephalitis.info/types-of-encephalitis/autoimmune-encephalitis/rasmussens-encephalitis/?utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&gad_source=1&gclid=CjwKCAiAxea5BhBeEiwAh4t5KykTABfw09g6k32MNWh15SkJT15FcK0QfVswkDoI6xtmCmxZTH50FxoC0n0QAvD_BwE)).  This is the (literal) 1 in 5 million possibility - an auto-immune disease that attacks the brain.  There's a spectrum of possibilities, but none of them are good.  If it's truly RE, then managing the spread/atrophy is top priority - i.e., she's likely to start regular IVIg infusions soon (Essentially, replenishing the body with extra antibodies and distracting it from attacking her brain) as well as a good bit of testing. 

The very thing we were hoping/praying against.  Perhaps our worst nightmare.  We're now working toward a second brain surgery to take more out.  We're terrified of what this means for the future.  Will this stop at a second surgery?  What deficits will she have afterward?  If it continues, we are aware that there's another surgery called a "Hemispherectomy" that could help (disconnecting one hemisphere of the brain from the other), but could also leave her weak or paralyzed on one side of her body, cause significant speech/language/cognitive impairments, and possible vision issues as well.  It has really messed me up mentally.  I just want my daughter.  It has me asking really dark questions like, "Is this the last Christmas, birthday, [fill in the blank], etc. that she'll be aware?" and "Are we going to have to choose at some point between her quality of life now vs. her longevity of life?" and "Will she still have her personality?" and "Will she able to talk to me in a year?" and "What about her singing, doing a cartwheel or handstand, playing basketball, etc.?".  

We received this news the same day Kayla and I were supposed to (finally) take a long weekend getaway down to New River Gorge National Park.  We had already canceled 2 other getaways due to developments in Lyla's situation over the course of the last year, and we struggled with whether or not to go this time as well.  But after seeing how excited both kiddos were to go visit grandparents, we decided to go anyway to attempt to process it all together. 

Our AirBnb was one that we had booked for Spring Break this past year as a family of 4, but had to cancel because Lyla was in the hospital and, as it happens, was the week our house was... ahem... cooked well done?  

Anyway, it was a bit of a struggle for me - there were a few elements (the flooring, the doors, etc.) that really reminded me of our crispy house, and Lyla would've really appreciated it.  She has said all along, "I just want the house back EXACTLY how it used to be".  It breaks my heart because she probably just wants SOMETHING in her life to be the way it used to be.  It also messed me up a bit being in a place that "felt" a little like the crispy house.  It reminded me of what life used to be like - normal.  Lyla's full health and personality.  Our house.  

It made me sad, and I cried.  A lot.

So we hiked as both therapy and something we both enjoy doing.  I have to admit that it was a beautiful metaphor for the journey we're currently on.  We walked hand in hand with tears in our eyes, stopping every now and again to notice various points of beauty - a vista, a lake, a rock formation, etc.  We also didn't know what's at the end of each trail.  Is it going to be a beautiful sunset?  Or perhaps there is no payoff at the end?  Thinking about this metaphor made it really difficult to take another literal step forward because what is "the end"?  What's there?  What does it look like?  

I don't want to move forward toward "the end" any more.  So I find myself sobbing.  A lot. 

The fall in West Virginia is incredible.  We missed peak fall colors, and it was still amazing.  A big takeaway was that there's a lot of beauty to be had in the death and falling of leaves.  I try not to think too far ahead, but if she were to come away from all of this with more deficits, there could potentially be beauty to be had in that... somehow.  

So we prayed... or maybe it looked more like sobbing on our knees and yelling hard to understand words through snotty noses and hard-to-catch breath, I don't know.  Splitting hairs, really.  All along, God has shown us that He's present and active throughout our story, both the garbage and the great.  That makes prayer hard.  How do I pray??  God never made any promises about Lyla's health.  Never made any promises about our house either.  I find myself handing over a "compliment sandwich" style prayer to God:

"God, I trust You with our daughter
Please, I BEG you to perform a miracle on her body in Jesus' name
Yet God... whatever path you choose, You're still good and I trust You."

If you are invested even the tiniest bit in her story, please commit to praying either a prayer of your own, or if you don't have the words, a similar prayer - for a miracle for her physically in Jesus' name, and that we will trust God no matter the outcome.  This is the single biggest way to help, and the thing that we believe has the greatest impact.  For a sense of community and unity around this, we'll be in prayer at exactly 12:00pm Eastern (Lunch time) every day.  Feel free to join in unity at either the same time or some other time throughout the day to really saturate the day in prayer.  God will do as He pleases to bring Him the most glory and honor, but we'll be pleading with Him to bring the most glory and honor while still keeping Lyla and her personality intact.

Speaking of prayer, Lyla's been getting into sitting in front of the Christmas tree in the morning and writing out prayers - a truly beautiful, unassisted thing.  I'll see if I can find a few pictures to attach in order to share her heart.  We're all doubling down on prayer, even Lyla.  Let's get after it together!

Back to the trip for a sec.  There came a point in time where we had just finished sobbing/praying/yelling and someone close to us texted a screenshot of scripture - Psalm 40:1-3

"I waited patiently for the Lord to help me, and he turned to me and heard my cry.  He lifted me out of the pit of despair, out of the mud and the mire.  He set my feet on solid ground and steadied me as I walked along.  He has given me a new song to sing, a hymn of praise to our God.  Many will see what he has done and be amazed.  They will put their trust in the Lord."

So relevent.  This ended up being our biggest takeaway split into 4 parts:
1. God heard our cry
2. No more despair 
3. Many will see what God has done
4. They will put their trust in Him because of it

This is what we keep coming back to because well... we kinda have to.  

Another bit of scripture that came up while hiking was from Psalm 23:4 (ICB version) - "Even if I walk through a very dark valley, I will not be afraid because You are with me. Your rod and Your shepherd's staff comfort me."  It was just something that came to mind during one of our hikes, and we said it out loud.  We found the reference a day later written on a handrail of a small bridge overlooking the main New River Gorge bridge.  What a beautiful discovery! 

That's it, y'all.  Sorry it isn't better news, but please... join us in prayer.  We're now smack dab in the middle of a 3-5 day EEG in Cleveland Clinic that started Monday, November 18th.  Also... be in  prayer that our house doesn't burn down while we're there this time. :)
